Marsha P. Johnson

"Pay It No Mind"

1945 — 1992

Marsha P. Johnson was a pioneering transgender activist, Stonewall veteran, and drag performer who fought tirelessly for LGBTQ+ rights and the most marginalized members of her community. Her warmth, courage, and unwavering dedication to justice made her a beloved figure and an icon of resistance.

Marsha at a pride demonstration in New York City, 1970s.

A Revolutionary Spirit

Born Malcolm Michaels Jr. in Elizabeth, New Jersey, Marsha arrived in New York City with $15 and a bag of clothes. She quickly became a prominent figure in Greenwich Village, known for her joyful spirit, elaborate outfits, and her commitment to helping those in need.

The "P" in her name stood for "Pay It No Mind," her response to questions about her gender. This phrase became a philosophy of resilience and self-acceptance that continues to inspire activists today.

Her Journey

Born in Elizabeth, New Jersey

Born Malcolm Michaels Jr., Marsha knew from an early age that she was different. She began wearing dresses at age five, despite facing harassment and discrimination.

Moved to New York City

After graduating high school, Marsha moved to Greenwich Village with just $15. She became a fixture in the neighborhood, performing as a drag queen and building community among other LGBTQ+ people.

Stonewall Uprising

Marsha was present during the historic Stonewall riots, a pivotal moment in LGBTQ+ history. Her participation in this watershed event helped spark the modern gay rights movement.

70
1970

Co-Founded STAR

Together with Sylvia Rivera, Marsha co-founded Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR), an organization dedicated to helping homeless transgender youth. They opened STAR House to provide shelter and support.

70s
1970s-80s

Activism & Community Work

Throughout these decades, Marsha continued her activism, participated in demonstrations, and supported countless LGBTQ+ youth. She was a founding member of ACT UP and fought for AIDS awareness and support.

92
1992

A Life Cut Short

Marsha's body was found in the Hudson River. Her death was initially ruled a suicide, but friends and activists believed she was murdered. The case was reopened in 2012 as a possible homicide, though it remains unsolved.

Her Enduring Legacy

★

Marsha is celebrated as a mother of the LGBTQ+ rights movement, inspiring generations of activists and transgender individuals.

★

A monument honoring Marsha and Sylvia Rivera was unveiled in Greenwich Village, the first public monument dedicated to transgender individuals.

★

Her life has been documented in films, books, and art, including the Netflix documentary "The Death and Life of Marsha P. Johnson."

Continue the Fight

Marsha's legacy lives on through the activists who continue to fight for transgender rights, LGBTQ+ equality, and justice for all marginalized communities.
